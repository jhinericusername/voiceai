# Interview Flow Summary

Top-level strategy: A highly standardized, lightly-technical screening call (~13-17 min) optimized for personality/founder-fit rather than deep coding skill. The interviewer (Prakul/Rakul, an engineer-recruiter at Weave/WorkWeave) follows a consistent funnel: rapport small talk -> self-intro/hobbies/motivation -> ONE technical 'hardest thing you built' deep dive (drilling for genuine difficulty and individual ownership) -> a battery of YC-derived behavioral questions (hacking a system, competitiveness cost, top-1% niche knowledge) -> candidate Q&A -> two logistics screens (5-day in-person SF, visa sponsorship). The interviewer explicitly frames most calls as 'getting to know you as a person.'

## Likely Core Question Sequence
This is the script-level sequence implied by the transcripts, normalized from the repeated question variants rather than sorted by frequency:

1. Hey, how are you? Where are you calling in from?
2. Could you tell me a little about yourself?
3. Can you recall a time when you solved a technical problem with a clever, novel, or hacky solution?
4. Have you ever applied to YC before?
5. The reason I ask is that there's a question on the YC application we really like. It's the same question I'd like to ask you: Can you tell me about a time you hacked a non-computer system to your advantage?
6. Can you tell me about an area of your life outside of work where you are extremely competitive?
7. Can you describe the single most extreme instance of you being competitive in this particular thing?
8. Can you tell me about a niche or obscure non-technical topic that nobody knows about and nobody cares about, but you are an expert in? Meaning you're in the top 1% of knowledge about this non-technical topic?

## Most Common Questions
- What is the hardest thing you've built (technically / outside of work)? (~86%)
- Tell me about a time your competitiveness cost you something / most extreme thing to win (~75%)
- Tell me about a time you hacked a non-computer system / broke the rules (~73%)
- Can you tell me a bit about yourself? (~68%)
- Where are you calling in from? (~68%)
- What is one niche topic you're unusually knowledgeable about (top 1%)? (~59%)
- Do you have any questions for me? (~80%)
- 5 days/week in-person SF + visa sponsorship logistics (~60-64%)

## Most Common Probe Triggers
- Abstract/high-level project answer -> 'what was the hardest part / oh-shit moment'
- Candidate uses 'we' or lists many components -> 'what was YOUR specific role?'
- Candidate makes a non-obvious technical claim -> 'why not the obvious approach?' / direct challenge
- Candidate names a niche interest (often hedging) -> 'are you top 1%? what have you done with it?'
- Candidate gives a generic competitiveness story -> 'what motivated you / why did you care?'
- Candidate stalls on the 'hacking a system' question -> interviewer supplies concrete scaffolding examples (sleep hacking, concert flyering)

## Signals Prioritized
- Agency / resourcefulness / willingness to bend rules (the YC 'hacking a system' question)
- Competitive drive and intensity, plus self-awareness of its costs
- Genuine technical depth and individual ownership (not team-credit or buzzwords)
- Intellectual curiosity / obsessive depth in some niche
- Authentic, company-specific or startup-specific motivation (vs. money/comfort)
- Honesty under challenge (notably in interview_010 and interview_050 where claims were stress-tested)
- Logistical fit: in-person SF willingness and work authorization

## Surprising Patterns
- The 'hardest thing you've built' is framed 'outside of work' in many later interviews, deliberately probing intrinsic motivation rather than just job competence.
- The YC-application check (CQ011) is almost never an end in itself — it functions as a deterministic setup for the 'hacked a system' question.
- Technical rigor varies dramatically: most calls accept the technical answer at face value, but a minority (interview_010, interview_050) become adversarial fact-checks, and in interview_010 the interviewer caught a candidate reading answers off a screen and ended early.
- Several 'interviews' inverted the dynamic: candidates were actually founders/not job-seeking (interview_013, interview_040, interview_041), turning the call into mentorship/networking rather than evaluation.
- Competitiveness is treated as a near-assumed trait ('if you want to work at a startup, you're competitive by nature'), which can lead the candidate toward a desired answer.
- The closing logistics pair (5-day SF in-person + visa) is remarkably scripted and consistent across interviewers.

## Low-Confidence Findings
- Exact frequencies are estimates: 6 transcripts (interview_015, 021, 022, 025, 032, 037, 044, 046) were empty or near-empty, so percentages are computed against 44 usable transcripts but reported against 50 where noted — treat all percentages as approximate.
- Whether the company-knowledge question (CQ005) is a deliberately retired part of the script or just interviewer-dependent is unclear; it concentrates in lower-numbered transcripts.
- The motivation follow-up to competitiveness (CQ014) and ownership probe (CQ009) appear in fewer than ~16% of interviews and may be under-counted due to inconsistent extraction labeling.
- Interviewer reasoning is inferred from question content and occasional explicit statements, not from stated rubrics (one transcript, interview_016, reveals a rubric: conscientiousness, agency, competitiveness, curiosity/production experience).
- Stage boundaries and 'typical_position' are interpretive; some interviews reorder behavioral and technical blocks.
- Two candidate names and several entities are transcription-approximate (ASR noise), so any verbatim quotes may differ slightly from intended phrasing.
